The job

What the website actually has to do.

Nobody finds a four-year practitioner training by accident. Students arrive through the community: a practitioner they trust, a workshop that moved them, a name passed along at the right moment. Which means the website has one job, and it happens late in the process. A referred prospect, already half-decided, sits down to do their due diligence. They read the program pages, they study the faculty, and they spend a long time on the fees page, because they are about to commit four years and $48,000 to this institute.

The site’s job is to hold them through that moment. It does not need to generate interest. It needs to be worthy of the trust the community has already built, at the exact moment someone is testing that trust against a major decision.

At this tuition, the math is simple. A single student reassured at that moment pays for this entire project several times over, and a single student quietly lost there costs far more than any website ever will.

The site today

What is holding the current site back.

The deepest issue is the foundation. The site runs on Mailchimp’s landing page builder, a side feature of an email marketing tool rather than a website platform. That choice made sense for getting something up quickly, and it has served its purpose, but it imposes ceilings that cannot be fixed in place. The platform provides no meta descriptions anywhere on the site, no real control over page titles, no structured data, and no way to tune performance. These are the signals search engines read, and they matter here in a specific way: a referral rarely arrives as a web address. It arrives as a name. Someone hears about Core Energetics at a workshop, or hears about this program from a practitioner they trust, and later that night they type what they remember into a search bar. Right now, that search comes up empty.

The site is reachable only by people who already have the exact address, and word of mouth almost never travels as an exact address.

Then there is the design itself. This is a program about embodiment, presence, and aliveness, and its website opens on a stock illustration: seven thin pages for a four-year decision. Prospective students feel that mismatch even if they never name it, and they feel it at exactly the moment they are deciding. When that moment ends, there is also nowhere to act: the site offers a contact form where an application should be.

The rebuild

What I propose to build.

A custom site of roughly seven pages: home, program and curriculum, faculty, dates and fees, workshops, testimonials, and a proper application and inquiry flow to replace the bare contact form. The design gets built around the work itself, with art direction guidance for photography of the training, the faculty, and the space at Earthdance, so that real imagery replaces stock illustration and the site begins to feel like the room the training happens in.

The rebuild also includes a simple typographic wordmark for the institute, carried through the site header, the favicon, and link previews. Today the site has no mark at all: the logo slot in the header sits empty, and the icon in the browser tab belongs to the website builder, not to Metamorphosis.

Underneath the design, the site gets the full technical foundation the current platform cannot provide: correct titles and meta descriptions on every page, structured data describing the institute, the program, and its location, a sitemap, and fast static hosting. The existing copy mostly carries over with editorial polish, so this is a redesign and a re-foundation rather than a rewrite of the institute’s voice.

The site moves onto hosting the institute owns. What I build is plain files, with no subscription platform underneath, so nobody can raise the rent on your own website or hold your content inside their editor. If you ever wanted to move it to another host or another developer, the whole site transfers over, no strings.

Design is environmental.

This program teaches, better than almost anyone, that people feel a space before they think about it: the body decides first. A website is read the same way. Prospective students will feel this site in the first few seconds, long before they weigh a single argument on it.

How the site feels is a preview of how the work feels.

The website is the first room of the training.
